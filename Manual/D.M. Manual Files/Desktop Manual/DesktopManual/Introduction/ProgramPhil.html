1. Program Philosophy:


Virtual Laboratories is committed to the development, production and distribution of effective simulation programs for the sciences which facilitate the transfer of information between the experimental and theoretical worlds. We believe that it is through the effective presentation of theoretical concepts applied in an interactive simulation of real world environments that the world of theory can find its greatest expression and utility.
Desktop Microscopist has been written to supply a broad range of computational and representational methods for the electron/x-ray microscopist and material scientist with the highest degree of flexibility, ease of use and user/program interactivity possible given current technology. We are dedicated to the continued development of Desktop Microscopist and to the development of other related programs for the physical sciences.

I.Descendent of Diffract


Although Desktop Microscopist is a direct descendant of Diffract ;v1.4 and other more vertically oriented programs, Desktop Microscopist differs from these previous programs in its utilization of the object oriented paradigm and in its greater integration of x-ray and electron diffraction theory. These two driving forces (object oriented programming and the integration of diffraction theory) from different disciplines have been synergistically integrated in the code development of Desktop Microscopist to create a flexible, easily expandable program which covers a large range of diffraction phenomena.

II. Object Orientation


The field of software engineering is perhaps the most rapidly changing technological field in the world today. This growth has been spurred by the accelerating improvement in computer hardware technology and the rapidly expanding user base which is becoming both more demanding and also highly interconnected. With this rapid expansion in computer capability and support base, code has become significantly more complex and difficult to manage, maintain and expand. It is through the use of object oriented techniques that we are seeking to circumvent these difficulties.

III. Merging Of Theoretical Fields


The theoretical similarity between different diffraction phenomena has been established since the early 1900's. It is possible to look at ionic, neutron, x-ray and electron diffraction as similar phenomena which adhere to the same rules. However, the specific qualities of a neutron versus x-ray and an x-ray versus electron implies significant differences in experimental technique and sensitivity to specific elements/material properties. Examples would be the use of vacuum and the need for lenses for electron diffraction versus x-ray diffraction, etc. The different high energy beam diffraction techniques have led to different nomenclature and a separation of theory and practice. The modern computer with its ability to store a vast amount of needed parameters and its rapid processing capabilities lends itself to the reunification of these disparate fields. Desktop Microscopist is the beginning of an effort to utilize the power of the personal computer to encourage this reunification.

IV. The Desktop Environment


Desktop Microscopist takes advantage of object oriented concepts to create a multi-windowed environment with a large number of different but strongly related objects. Each object supports a specific type of calculation restricted by specific physical assumptions. These physical assumptions and/or limits can continuously be refined in subsequently derived objects. It is expected that these benefits will allow Desktop Microscopist to expand into a number of areas including image simulation and dynamical theory of CBED and HOLZ patterns.

V. Future Directions(See New With Version 2.0)

In addition to the strong support of pattern simulation, Desktop Microscopist is beginning to support the direct comparison of calculated with theoretical results. To this end, Desktop Microscopist allows the user to import ;and/or grab in real time patterns which can be subsequently analyzed for pertinent data. Desktop Microscopist has also begun to address the question of data access and comparison (data base functionality). It is possible to search for and retrieve crystals from the Electron Diffraction Database, the JCPDF Powder Diffraction Files, the NIST Crystal Database and Desktop Microscopist crystal files. The Electron Diffraction Database, the Powder Diffraction Data base and the NIST Crystal Database are available separately; contact Virtual Laboratories for details.
